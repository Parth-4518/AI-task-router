## 1. HISTORICAL BACKGROUND AND CAUSES

### The Strait of Hormuz
- Geography: Waterway between the Persian Gulf and the Gulf of Oman
- North coast: Iran
- South coast: Musandam Peninsula (Oman / UAE)
- Length: ~104 miles (167 km); Width: 60 mi to 24 mi (97 km to 39 km)
- Provides the ONLY sea passage from the Persian Gulf to the open ocean
- One of the world's most strategically important choke points

### Pre-2026 Context
- Iran and Israel became adversaries after the 1979 Iranian Revolution
- Iran severed ties with Israel on 18 February 1979
- Decades of proxy conflict through Hezbollah, Hamas, and Iranian-backed militias
- Iran's nuclear program and ballistic missile development escalated tensions
- Before 2026, the strait had NOT generally been closed for extended periods during Middle East conflicts (unlike Straits of Tiran/Bab-el-Mandeb)
- Iran had occasionally threatened to close the strait but never did so for long

### Escalation Timeline
- **2023:** Middle Eastern crisis began
- **2024:** Iran and Israel exchanged direct missile strikes; Iran seized MSC Aries container ship in Strait of Hormuz
- **June 2025:** "Twelve-Day War" — Israeli strikes on Iranian military/nuclear facilities; US airstrikes on Iranian nuclear sites; ceasefire followed
- **January 2026:** Iranian security forces massacred thousands of civilians during crackdown on largest protests since 1979
- **President Trump** threatened military action and began largest US military buildup in region since 2003

## 3. MILITARY SITUATION AND FORCES INVOLVED

### Iran (IRGC + Armed Forces)
- Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei assassinated (28 Feb 2026)
- Naval officer Alireza Tangsiri (overseeing blockade) assassinated by Israel
- Capabilities: Missiles, drones, naval vessels, sea mines, asymmetric warfare
- Tactics: Boarding merchant ships, laying mines, missile/drone strikes on bases
- IRGC controls the strait closure and maritime interdiction operations

### United States
- Launched surprise airstrikes (28 Feb 2026)
- Conducting "Project Freedom" aerial campaign to reopen strait
- Naval blockade of Iranian ports (since 13 Apr 2026)
- Largest military buildup in region since 2003 Iraq War
- Operating against Iranian naval vessels and mine-laying boats

### Israel
- Co-launched initial strikes with US
- Assassinated key Iranian officials including Khamenei and Tangsiri
- Targeting Iranian military and nuclear facilities

### Gulf Allies (UAE, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait)
- Responding with their own strikes on Iran and regional allies
- Affected by Hormuz closure (their primary maritime export route)
- Hosting US military bases targeted by Iran

### Impact on Shipping
- Tanker traffic dropped ~70% initially
- Subsequently dropped to ~zero
- 150+ ships anchored outside strait avoiding risks
- Major shipping firms suspended operations

## 4. ECONOMIC IMPACT (Oil Trade & Shipping)

### Pre-Crisis Hormuz Trade Volumes (2023-2025)
- **25%** of world's seaborne oil trade passed through annually
- **20%** of world's liquefied natural gas (LNG) passed through annually
- Major route for petroleum products to Europe and Asia
- Critical for Europe's energy supply
- ONLY maritime route for UAE, Qatar, Bahrain, Kuwait, and Iraq

### Post-Closure Impact
- Complete disruption of Gulf oil/LNG exports
- Severe supply shortages for dependent nations
- Global energy price spike (implied, though specific figures not in research)
- Shipping insurance costs skyrocketed
- Alternative routes (if any) are extremely limited and costly

### Affected Countries' Dependencies
- UAE, Qatar, Bahrain, Kuwait, Iraq: ONLY maritime oil export route
- Europe: Critical energy supply line disrupted
- Asia: Major petroleum import route affected

### Strait Status

## 6. INTERNATIONAL RELATIONS AND DIPLOMATIC EFFORTS

### Failed Diplomacy
- **Islamabad Talks:** Ceasefire negotiations failed by 13 April 2026
- Surprise attacks launched DURING ongoing US-Iran nuclear negotiations
- Assassination of Supreme Leader severely degraded diplomatic channels

### US Position
- Military action prioritized over diplomacy initially
- Temporary operational pause (6 May) suggests exploring negotiated settlement
- Maintaining naval blockade as leverage
- "Complete and final agreement" language implies comprehensive demands

### Iranian Position
- Retaliatory closure of Hormuz as asymmetric response
- Continued missile/drone strikes despite losses
- Internal crackdown on protests (January 2026) preceded war
- Leadership decapitation (Khamenei) creates succession uncertainty

### Regional Dynamics
- Gulf states (UAE, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait) actively participating militarily
- Shift from proxy conflict to direct multi-state war
- Risk of broader regional escalation

### Global Implications
- Major energy supply disruption affecting Europe and Asia
- Potential for global economic recession due to oil price shocks
- Testing of US extended deterrence commitments
- Question of whether Hormuz can be militarily reopened against Iranian resistance

## SUMMARY

The 2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis represents the most severe disruption of this critical chokepoint in modern history. What began as a US-Israeli preemptive strike campaign against Iran has escalated into a multi-state war with global economic consequences. The closure of Hormuz — through which 25% of seaborne oil and 20% of LNG flows — threatens energy security for Europe, Asia, and Gulf states themselves. Military efforts to reopen the strait are ongoing but face determined Iranian resistance using asymmetric tactics (mines, drones, missile strikes). A temporary US operational pause in early May 2026 suggests a potential diplomatic opening, but no ceasefire is in place and the strait remains effectively closed.
